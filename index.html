Many over one

any languages over one language, many paradigms over one paradigm. This is object-oriented and functional. Underlying concepts are different, but it's clearly a map, an important concept. Additionally, another important concept here: lambdas, which are very modern and don't have an unified syntax, but still an important concept to learn.

Practical over theoretical.

What is a map? You can explain to hell and beyond what it's about, and still not understand it until you put it to work in practice. When should you use a map, when a loop?

MVP over "exercises", "written exams" and "problems".

It's admittedly difficult to create a minimally viable "product" if all you have are literals, but well, literals can be complicated too. Lambdas are literal, for instance.

Understand and overcome errors over avoid errors.

When you work with different languages, it's unavoidable that errors show up. You need to look at them, and interpret them, to find what's wrong with the syntax. Errors feel literally wrong, but also they make people feel bad, because they thing they're stupid and are not following instructions correctly. Errors are simply part of the learning process. This image is quite usual in my cloud computing class. When something is wrong, students are not used to look at the error, interpret it and make it right; they simply think they don't know how to do something. That's bad, and wrong, and is a hurdle for learning.
